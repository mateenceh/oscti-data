Pirate Bay co-founder Peter Sunde arrested in Sweden

02 Jun 2014 21 Law & order

Post navigation

Previous: Say hello to your vending machine – it might be watching you!
Next: NSA facial recognition program scours web for images to identify suspects
by Lee Munson

Pirate Bay co-founder Peter Sunde has been arrested in southern Sweden, according to Reuters.

Sunde (aka “Brokep”) was convicted of aiding copyright infringement in 2009 and sentenced to a year of jail time, plus a hefty fine.

The amount of prison time was later reduced to eight months but Sunde went on the run, and Interpol have been looking for him ever since.

The former Pirate Bay spokesman has reportedly been living in Berlin but still has family in Sweden and was arrested on Saturday at a farm near Malmö in the county of Skåne.

The news was confirmed in a report from Swedish newspaper Expressen in which National Police press secretary Carolina Ekeus said, “He will serve his prison sentence.” The paper also reports that local law enforcement worked in collaboration with Polish police.

His apprehension may have come as a surprise to Sunde though who, only last month, told Ars Technica that he thought it unlikely he would be taken into custody:

It's quite easy to cross borders in Europe without any controls. And the Nordic Union, don't forget - the Nordic countries has been passport-free since ages before Schengen [an agreement which effectively removed passport control with the European Union].

Despite facing a stint behind bars and a fine of 46 million Swedish krona ($6.9 million, £4.1 million), Sunde also told Ars that he wasn’t even sure whether there was an outstanding arrest warrant awaiting him in Sweden.

Peter Althin, the lawyer retained by Sunde during his trial, believes his client will now have to serve out his sentence, telling Swedish news service TT:

He is extremely talented and I still think that the judgement was wrong.

It's about being on the cutting edge if one is going to be successful... But if one is too far ahead it is not always about success. Peter fought for file-sharing and in 10 years I think it goes without saying that file-sharing for one's own needs will be allowed.

Sunde and other key personnel behind the Pirate Bay had attempted to fight their convictions for some time. After the Swedish Supreme Court declined to hear their appeal in February 2012 they took their case to the European Court of Human Rights which subsequently dismissed the case, saying that it was “inadmissible”. A further appeal to the Swedish Supreme Court was then also rejected.

Thus far, only Gottfrid Svartholm and Carl Lundström have spent time in jail. Svartholm was detained in Cambodia in late 2012 before being deported to Sweden where he now faces another year in prison in connection with an unrelated hacking charge.

A fourth defendant, Fredrik Neij, remains at large, reportedly residing in Laos with his wife.

The Pirate Bay site itself, which was launched in 2003, provided links which connected users to music and movie files that were stored on each others’ computers. Such a sharing strategy prompted copyright holders to take the company to court, citing lost revenue as a cause for seeking damages.

While the website, now under new ownership and registered in the Seychelles, is still up and running, it continues to be blocked in many countries, including the UK.
