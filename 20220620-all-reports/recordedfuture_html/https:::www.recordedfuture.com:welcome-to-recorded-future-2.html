Welcome to Recorded Future

June 19, 2009 • Jason Hines

Introduction

What is Recorded Future?

Recorded Future allows you to
explore temporal data about people, companies, places and events
where these entities occur. Interested in what led up to the General
Motors bankruptcy? What will happen to Apple next month? Where did Hu
Jintao travel recently? Where is he traveling in the future? Basically,
Recorded Future news analytics allow you to analyze, search, explore, understand all
aspects of time and events.

We harvest thousands of sources (everything from government websites
to SEC filings to mainstream and niche media to blogs and twitter
feeds) for time and event data and compile it into one massive,
carefully
organized and equally carefully ranked, database of time
oriented information.

You can search and explore this data to find patterns, clusters,
outliers, and anomalies – or perhaps generate data sets that you want
to map, visualize, and put to use in statistical analysis. We’d like to
think that the opportunities are endless.

What is Recorded Future not?

Recorded Future is not a Google
or Bing or
href=”http://www.yahoo.com”>Yahoo like search engine (or
href=”http://www.wolframalpha.com/”>Wolfram Alpha for that sake).
Type Pfizer to find the Pfizer website and you’ll be disappointed. Type
your own name to find what websites reference you are referenced in and
you’ll be disappointed. Type in a movie name to find show times and you
will be disappointed. But, if you’d like to find out about upcoming
events regarding Pfizer or what the CEO of Pfizer has said recently
regarding the FDA, you’ll be excited!

Basics

What sort of data is in Recorded Future?

At it’s core Recorded Future organize Events and the Entities they
involve, and when they will, are, or did happen. Basically – events
like IPOs, bankruptcies, mergers, management turnover, and time points
associated with them. In particular we try to find the actual time
something did happen, is happening, or will happen in the future (as
opposed to simple publication time).

Entities fall into a series of categories – such as Companies (IBM,
Google, etc), Organizations (United
Nations, World Bank, etc.), People (Barack Obama, Hu Jintao, etc.), and
Places (China, London) – but also Technologies (3G, WiMax), Diseases
(Malaria, H1N1 flu), etc. You can search on any of these using the
entity field.

Events (and facts) include for example Bankruptcies, Analyst
Ratings,
Credit Ratings, Quotations, IPOs, Mergers, Acquisitions, etc. See the
href=”#EE”>Events and Entities section below.  Best way
to explore is the auto fill menu in the Event field of the main user
interface. Events typically involve one or more entities – i.e. a
Bankruptcy includes a Company, whereas a Merger will include multiple
Companies.

We do a lot of smart things to find time points in text allowing us
to find things like “filing for bankruptcy next week”, “option expiring
in two weeks”, or “product to be launched in 2011”. This forms the
center piece of our handling of time.

Recorded Future also store many other sorts of data, such
publication time, ranking of the event and the entity, source, source
type, etc.

Ranking

Recorded Future continuously ranks all items in the database – events
and entities, with the goal of being able to provide users with the
most relevant hits in each time period displayed. This ranking is done
according to a series of metrics and variables, based on a patented
algorithm.

What does the interface look like?

You’ll first be displayed a login screen


style=”border: 0px solid ; width: 486px; height: 54px;”>

Type in your user name and password to get started. You will have
selected your user name and password through the registration process.

There are a series of text fields you can use to search. The core
one is the Entities field, which can be used to search for example for
companies or cities. Start typing and Recorded Future will auto fill on
companies, people, and places. For companies and places it will also
display their industries and specific places in case there are multiple
matches (e.g. Stockholm, Sweden vs. Stockholm, Minnesota). In addition
to companies, people, and places you may also search for organizations,
technologies, diseases, and a series of other types of information.

You can easily explore many types of events by using the Event
search field. The easiest way to try is to use the autofill menu, just
start typing to see what’s possible. You can also combine the Entities
and Event fields, for example to find acquisition events involving
Pfizer.

Click the little arrow on the right in the text field to just
explore the menu.

Some event types might modify the search interface to allow for more
detailed queries – for example selecting the Quotation query will show
a field where you can specify a text string about what a person has
said – i.e. in the example below you’re searching for comments by Steve
Ballmer regarding Yahoo.

Likewise you can explore many types of time expression. These are
most meaningful when combined with entity and event searches – to find
events like Pfizer next month or Product launch next year. Click the
little arrow on the right in the text field to just explore the menu.

Results may be presented in multiple forms as can be seen below.

Text view is similar to a classic search engine view, but organized
in clusters of time. Each cluster can typically be expanded (if there
are more results) or collapsed. Each group is sorted by time and the
items displayed in the group are those with the highest rank.

Calendar view overlays search results on a calendar view, with each
day having a color intensity after the number of hits for that day.

Finance view overlays search results on the Google Finance chart.

Timeline produces an interactive timeline that may be scrolled and
zoomed.

The RSS button generates a new web page with a RSS feed
corresponding to the query just performed. The iCal button generates an
iCal file that may be imported into Google Calendar, iCal, etc. with
the benefit of dynamically populating your calendar with the events of
your query. The Excel button generates a csv file of your data.

Exploring Recorded Future

To get you started with what Recorded Future can do we’ll use a
series of examples that will give you a sense of the capabilities.
Start by typing Adobe in the entity field. Underneath the Adobe name
you see the SIC industry code for adobe – in this case Prepackaged
software.

Also try ADBE – the adobe stock ticker. Works equally well to reach the
Adobe company name.

Then try Adobe Future, by adding Future in the Time field.

Finally we may want to search on an event involving Adobe – for example
to find future product release of Adobe select ProductRelease Adobe
Future

Going back to Adobe Future, clicking Search will generate search
results matching Adobe in the Future – which means finding events
involving Adobe in the future (or in time ranges overlapping with the
current time) or entity hits on Adobe (as a company) in the future. The
items in each time bucket are the most highly ranked in that time
period.

The standard result view is a text view – but there are a series of
them avaiable. The text result view is organized into a series of time
buckets – each of with may be open and closed with by clicking the date
denominating the bucket.

Each individual result item may also be reviewed by clicking the time
point/range for the item

saying

And if we want to be more specific, and for example find out what
Shantanu Narayen is saying about Flash we use the Quotation event with
the text “flash” added

Events and Entities in Recorded Future

  • What sort of entities does Recorded Future support?
    • You can search for people (Barack Obama), places (United
      States), and companies (AstraZeneca) with the auto fill menu in the
      Entities field. If you type something that’s not matched in auto fill,
      your search will be a “free text” search into fields for organizations,
      technologies, medical conditions, medical treatments, etc.
  • What sort of events does Recorded Future record? We record a
    whole range of events – many financial but also many others – and we
    think this should give you a wide set of opportunities to search,
    understand, explore, model the world of events as they unfold. Below is
    a list of event types – and obviously we’re continuously working
    intensively to expand the set of events.

    • Financial market events: IPO (company going
      public), Buybacks (company buying back shares), CompanyListingChange
      (company changing listing), SecondaryIssuance (company issuing equity
      in secondary market), BonusSharesIssuance (company issuing bonus
      shares), StockSplit (company doing a stock split), Dividend (company
      issuing dividends)
    • Company meetings: ConferenceCall (company
      holding a public conference call), CompanyMeeting (company meeting –
      e.g. annual meeting)
    • Business relations: Acquisition (company
      acquiring other company), Merger (two companies merging), JointVenture
      (two companies entering into a joint venture), Alliance (two companies
      entering into an alliance), BusinessRelation
      (two companies entering into a business relation), CompanyAffiliates
      (two companies being affiliated)
    • Earnings, ratings, and guidance:
      CompanyEarningsAnnouncement (company announcing earnings),
      CompanyEarningsGuidance (company giving guidance on earnings),
      AnalystEarningsEstimate (analyst providing estimate on company’s
      earnings), AnalystRecommendation (sell side analyst providing
      recommendation on company), CreditRating (credit rating agency
      providing recommendation on a company’s credit)
    • Products, technology, and product usage:
      ProductRelease (company launching product), ProductIssues (product
      having issues), ProductRecall (product being recalled), CompanyCustomer
      (company being customer of product/customer), CompanyUsingProduct
      (company using product), CompanyProduct (company being product
      developer), CompanyTechnology (company being technology developer)
    • Expansion/change and investments:
      CompanyExpansion (company expanding), CompanyInvestment (company
      investing in other company), CompanyNameChange (company changing name)
    • Company problems/issues: CompanyLayoffs
      (company doing layoffs), CompanyLaborIssues (company having labor
      issues), CompanyReorganization (company reorganizing), DebtFinancing
      (company doing debt financing), CompanyLegalIssues (company having
      legal issues), CompanyForceMajeure (company facing force majeure
      issue), Bankruptcy (company in bankruptcy), CompanyAccountingChange
      (company changing accounting principles/policies), CompanyRestatement
      (company restating financial statements, DelayedFiling (company
      delaying filing of financial statements)
    • Management change: EmploymentChange (person
      changing jobs – typically executive/management person),
      EmploymentRelation (person being having employment relation with
      company)
    • Personal relations: PersonCommunication
      (person communicating with other person), PersonRelation (person having
      relation with other person, PersonTravel (person traveling),
      FamilyRelation (person having family relation), PersonCareer (person
      having job/career), PersonEducation (person having/being awarded
      education)
    • Communication: Quotation (person being
      quotated saying something in a news/media/internet source)
    • Patents: PatentFiling, PatentIssuance
    • Pharmaceutical industry: FDAPhase
    • Political relationships/facts:
      PoliticalRelationship (political relationship between people)
    • Judicial: Indictment (indictment of person),
      Arrest (arrest of person), Trial (person on trial in court), Conviction
      (conviction of person in court)
    • Disasters: ManMadeDisaster, NaturalDisaster
    • Environmental: EnvironmentalIssue, Extinction
    • Entertainment: MovieRelease,
      MusicAlbumRelease

You might run into that our source coverage in some cases is such that
some event types are less meaningful or have less coverage. One example
would be PatentFiling where our sources are such that we don’t have
very good coverage for this event type right now. Watch this space
though!

What content does Recorded Future index?

We carefully go through thousands of sources to find interesting
events and entities and time references – from all over the world.
Content sources include government sites, mainstream and niche media,
blogs, twitter streams, etc. When we find something interesting we
carefully record it and equally carefully keep the reference to the
underlying source materials.

Recorded Future indexes a whole range of content. At it’s core are
entities – such as companies (IBM, Google, etc), organizations (United
Nations, World Bank, etc.), people (Barack Obama, Hu Jintao, etc.), and
places (China, London) – but also technologies (3G, WiMax), Diseases
(Malaria, H1N1 flu), etc.

On top of that we also index events – Bankruptcies, Analyst Ratings,
Credit Ratings, Quotations, IPOs, Mergers, Acquisitions, etc. Best way
to explore is the auto fill menu in the Event field of the main user
interface.

Obviously we then also index time – carefully harvesting documents,
sources, feeds for direct and indirect references to temporal
information. Again, best way to explore is the auto fill menu in the
Event field of the main user interface.

It’s important to remember though that we’re not an ordinary
document/keyword search engine like Google
or Bing. If you try to search for
information that we don’t index you won’t find anything. It might be
fun to try to find out what Britney Spears is doing next month – but
you’re unlikely to find anything (but hey – give it a try!).

Obviously we’re working hard to add more and more content. Watch
this space!
