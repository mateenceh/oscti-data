Botnets drop banking Trojans, start distributing ransomware instead 

Botnets are ditching banking Trojans in favour of distributing ransomware, according to researchers from Group-IB.

At its recent CyberCrimeCon 2020 virtual conference, the security company said that the four largest banking botnets run by Russian speakers - Trickbot, Dridex, Qbot, and Silent Night - have shifted from distributing banking Trojans to distributing ransomware.

This is unsurprising when you consider that Group-IB also revealed that, based on known attacks, the 14 biggest ransomware gangs made at least $1 billion between the second half of 2019 and the first half of 2020. Their true profits - including attacks that havenât been publicly reported - are likely to be much higher. 

Maze, Sodinokibi, Ryuk, Netwalker, and DoppelPaymer are the most active ransomware operations in the period it was looking at, the security firm reported.

DDoS attacks targeting online retailers quadrupled since start of COVID-19 pandemic

The number of distributed denial of service (DDoS) attacks targeting e-commerce websites has increased four-fold since the start of the COVID-19 pandemic compared to the same period last year, according to research from Stormwall.

Stormwall attributes the rise in attacks to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to more people shopping online, and also attackers using DDoS attacks as leverage to extort money from businesses. âCyber-criminals use website downtime as a leverage, promising to stop the attack and restore the service operation, once the victim company pays the ransom,â the company said.

A surge in home remodelling and decorating during the pandemic has also meant that retailers in certain sectors are particularly attractive to cyber criminals. Attacks on online electronics stores increased five-fold, the number of attacks on online furniture stores increased eight-fold, and attacks aimed at online renovation stores grew seven-fold.

The incident occured when a hospital employee uploaded a spreadsheet with usernames, passwords, and access keys to sensitive government systems onto GitHub this month. Among the systems that had credentials exposed were two government databases used to store data on COVID-19 patients.

The details on the databases included information such as patient names, addresses, and ID information, as well as healthcare records such as medical history and medication regimes. Personal information about the president's family, seven government ministers, and the governors of 17 Brazilian states was also included in the database.

The issue came to light when a GitHub user spotted the spreadsheet and reported it to the media. It has now been removed from GitHub and officials say they have secured their systems.
